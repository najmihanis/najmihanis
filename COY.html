Overview
What
  • Developed a waist-supported crutch system focused on stability, comfort, and hands-free mobility.
  • Designed and built a functional MVP using 3D-printed components, aluminum tubing, and a weightlifting belt anchor with a 2-DOF joint system.
  • Addressed key recovery challenges through iterative design, prototyping, and user feedback.
How
  • Conducted customer discovery with physical therapists, orthopedic surgeons, and stakeholders to identify recovery needs, gait issues, and product requirements.
  • Developed three design iterations using mood boards, sketching, CAD modeling, and rendering to converge on the final concept.
  • Prototyped custom joints and handles with 3D printing and integrated adjustable pin slots into aluminum tubing.
  • Researched and tested walking and load-bearing mechanisms to reduce technical risk.
  • Performed cost analysis, identified target markets, assessed business risks, and created a pitch deck to support startup viability.
The Problem

Conventional crutches shift body load to the hands and shoulders, forcing users into compensatory gait patterns that increase fatigue and instability.

  • High upper-body strain over long recovery periods
  • Poor balance and reduced confidence while walking
  • Limited ability to carry objects or perform daily tasks

Crutch On You explores waist-based load transfer while preserving a repeatable walking trajectory and adjustable fit across recovery phases.

Ideation

Early sketches explored how a waist-supported crutch could reproduce useful walking motion without relying on the circular swing of traditional crutches. Concepts focused on alternative mechanisms such as spring-slider joints, multi-bar linkages, and cam-driven systems with damping. The exploration centered on joint placement, walking trajectories, and keeping the system compact while remaining stable and adaptable across recovery phases.

Risk Reduction

Prototype 01 — Load & Stability

The first prototype focused on understanding how a waist-supported system distributes load on static conditions.

This was done to surface the riskiest assumptions early, validate them through testing, and reduce risk before committing to more complex motion.

Prototype 02 — Joint & User Experience

The second prototype focused on the 2-DOF joint mechanism, exploring how the joint behaves in motion, how it feels to the user, and how loads act on the structure while walking.

This validated that the system could stay supportive beyond static use.
